About

I'm a UX Designer with 10 years of experience building internal software at O'Reilly Auto Parts — mobile apps, point of sale, desktop tools, and everything in between. I care deeply about the people on the other end of the screen. Not as users in a persona document, but as real people whose days get a little harder or a little easier depending on whether the software they're stuck with was designed thoughtfully or not.

That's the job to me. Not shipping features. Not checking boxes. Making something someone can pick up, use without frustration, and maybe — if we did it right — not even think about. Because good design gets out of the way.

I've spent my career designing for the people who don't always get a seat at the table — the store team member doing inventory between other tasks, the delivery driver who needs information fast and has no time for friction. I want the underdog to have a voice, and I think design is one of the best tools we have for giving it to them.

I'm a lifelong nerd who finally found her people in tech. Turns out the curiosity, the obsessive attention to detail, the need to understand how everything works — those aren't liabilities in this field. They're the job. I bring that same energy to everything I do — whether that's sweating the details of a component, wrangling two small kids, or digging past the symptoms to find the real problem worth solving.

Cycle Count & Toolbox App

Replacing a paper-based inventory process across 5,000+ stores with a purpose-built mobile scanning experience.

O'Reilly Auto Parts
Zebra Mobile (Android)
UX Designer, Research, Delivery
$17M+ in year-one payroll savings
The Problem

Across more than 5,000 O'Reilly Auto Parts stores, monthly inventory cycle counts were a paper-based process. Someone — a team member, a delivery driver between runs, a manager scrambling at month's end — would print crowded sheets from the POS system, walk the store manually counting parts, write everything down by hand, and pass the paper to a manager to validate.

The manager then had to do the mental math themselves: if the system showed 5 oil filters but only 3 were counted, they had to calculate the adjustment (-2) and manually enter it into the POS — the system didn't accept a simple "the correct number is 3."

Completed paper sheets had to be physically stored for 90+ days in case a loss prevention auditor came calling. The whole process was slow, error-prone, and fell on whoever happened to have a free moment.

My Role

UX Designer on a small internal team. I led design from research through delivery, working alongside a product analyst and engineering team. This was an early UX effort at O'Reilly — formal research tools weren't yet established — so I did what I could: store visits, shadowing team members doing live paper counts, and direct conversations with people in the field. I completed cycle counts myself to experience the friction firsthand.

The Challenge

This was an early UX effort inside a company that hadn't yet invested in formal research tools or processes. We worked with what we had — store visits, shadowing, firsthand experience — and designed something that scaled to thousands of locations. Getting there meant navigating heavy stakeholder requirements while still advocating for what team members actually needed on the floor.

The Outcome
$17M+
in payroll savings — first year after launch

The success of the app also drove a significant hardware investment — stores were provisioned with additional Zebra devices as the program scaled, a direct signal that the tool had earned its place in daily operations.

Beyond the number, team members gained something that fit how they actually worked — on a shared device, on the move, in the aisles. Cycle counts went from a dreaded end-of-month scramble to a routine task anyone could pick up and complete.

The app has since grown. Toolbox now houses multiple productivity tools, and Cycle Count has been updated to include back room stock mapping, driven by continued user feedback after launch. Fully eliminating the paper process is now a new project on my plate — which speaks to the ongoing, iterative nature of designing for large-scale internal operations.

Drift Design System

The Problem

O'Reilly Auto Parts' internal product suite has grown for decades without a unified design language. The current point of sale system alone is a patchwork of legacy technology — multiple frameworks, inconsistent error handling, mismatched colors, and UI patterns that vary wildly from screen to screen.

With a modernized POS system on the horizon and a four-person design team responsible for an enormous surface area — catalog, mobile productivity apps, fleet management, point of sale, item sourcing, and more — the absence of a shared foundation wasn't just a design problem. It was a velocity problem.

This wasn't a new issue. Years earlier, while on the mobile team, I helped build an informal UI kit for our internal apps. It was scrappy and self-initiated — no official investment, no engineering alignment — but it worked. It made us faster and more consistent. That experience made it clear: a real design system wasn't a nice-to-have. It was a requirement.

The Challenge

Drift has never had ideal conditions. Engineering and product have historically operated in silos, and getting organizational buy-in for foundational work — the kind that doesn't ship a feature — is a constant negotiation. Phase 1 has been built scrappily, between other priorities, by a small team that believes in the work even when the organization hasn't fully caught up.

That tension is real and worth naming. A design system only has value if it gets used, and adoption requires trust across disciplines that has to be earned over time.

Where It Stands

Phase 1 is wrapping up. Drift currently houses a full foundations layer — color tokens, typography, iconography, elevation, layout, and logos — alongside a growing component library of atoms: buttons, badges, banners, dividers, loading indicators, links, menus, selection controls, sheets, snackbars, and tooltips.

It serves today as a shared source of truth for the design team — a common language for evaluating new work, even before engineering fully adopts it. Next steps for Phase 2 are being defined now.
